{@Caru@}¦ designates a ‘kettle’ or ‘pot’ from the Rigveda
onwards.<sup>1</sup> It had a lid ({%apidhāna%}) and hooks ({%aṅka%}) by which
it could be hung over a fire.<sup>2</sup> It was made of iron or bronze<sup>3</sup>
[Page1-257+ 56]
({%ayasmaya%}). The word is also secondarily used<sup>4</sup> to denote the
contents of the pot, the mess of grain which was cooked in it.
<F>1) Rv. i. 7, 6; vii. 104, 2; ix. 52, 3;
x. 86, 18; 167, 4; Av. iv. 7, 4; ix. 5, 6;
xi. 1, 16; 3, 18; xviii. 4, 16 {%et seq.,%} etc.
It is called {%pañca-bild,%} ‘with five open-
ings,’ in the Taittirīya Saṃhitā, i. 6,
1, 2; Kāṭhaka Saṃhitā, v. 6; xxxii. 6;
Maitrāyaṇī Saṃhitā, i. 4, 4. 9, etc.</F>
<F>2) Rv. i. 162, 13; Av. xviii. 4, 53.</F>
<F>3) Satapatha Brāhmaṇa, xiii. 3, 4, 5

Aitareya Brāhmaṇa, i. 1; Śatapatha
Brāhmaṇa, i. 7, 4, 7; ii. 5, 3, 4; iii. 2,

9, 216.</F>
