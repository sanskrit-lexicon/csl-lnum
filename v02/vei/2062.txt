{@Prāṇa,@}¦ properly denoting ‘breath,’ is a term of wide and
vague significance in Vedic literature. It is frequently men-
tioned from the Rigveda<sup>1</sup> onwards; in the Āraṇyakas and
Upaniṣads it is one of the commonest symbols of the unity of
the universe.<sup>2</sup> In the narrow sense Prāṇa denotes one of the
vital airs, of which five are usually<sup>3</sup> enumerated — Prāṇa,
{@Apāna, Vyāna, Udāna,@} and {@Samāna;@} but often only two,
Prāṇa and Apāna,<sup>4</sup> or Prāṇa and Vyāna,<sup>5</sup> or Prāṇa and Udāna;<sup>6</sup>
or three, Prāṇa, Apāna, and Vyāna,<sup>7</sup> or Prāṇa, Udāna, and
Vyāna,<sup>8</sup> or Prāṇa, Udāna, and Samāna;<sup>9</sup> or four, Prāṇa,
Apāna, Vyāna, and Samāna,<sup>10</sup> or Prāṇa, Apāna, Udāna,
Vyāna.<sup>11</sup> The exact sense of each of these breaths when all
are mentioned cannot be determined.<sup>12</sup>
