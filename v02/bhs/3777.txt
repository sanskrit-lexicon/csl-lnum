In the canonical <lang>Pali</lang> texts, <ab>e.g.</ab> <ls>DN</ls> 〔ii.137.3〕 (<ab>cf.</ab> <ab>comm.</ab> 〔ii.572—573〕) yena Kusinārā upavattanaṃ Mallānaṃ sālavanaṃ ten(a) …, and <ab>cf.</ab> <ab>esp.</ab> <ls>Dpv.</ls> 〔15.70〕 Kusinārāyaṃ bhagavā Mallānaṃ upavattane, it could be understood [Page141-a] as {%the country%} (of the Mallas); but the <ab>comms.</ab> seem to have taken it as a place-name, specifically the name of the śāl-grove where the Buddha died. And this is perhaps confirmed by one passage, <ls>Dpv.</ls> 〔6.19〕 yadā ca parinibbāyi saṃbuddho Upavattane, where the <lex>gen.</lex> Mallānaṃ is not found, and Upa° most naturally would be a <ab>n.</ab> of a place (but see below). In Akanumaʼs Dictionary of the Proper Names of Indian Buddhism the word is misquoted as Upavattava = <lang>Skt.</lang> °vartava (but the Chinese transcriptions quoted end consistently in -tan, supporting °vartana); the <lang>Chin.</lang> translations seem regularly to interpret it as a common noun, {%uncultivated land%} (the word for {%land%} usually rendering bhūmi, {%land country%}). This may be interpreted as support for use as a common noun, as it is used (rarely) in <lang>Skt.</lang> Note that in <lang>BHS</lang> the name of the grove is clearly {@Yamakaśāla(ka)vana@}, <ab>q.v.</ab>; not so in <lang>Pali</lang>. Modern editors and interpreters seem unanimously to take <lang>Pali</lang> Upavattana as <ab>n.</ab> of the grove (see <ls>DPPN</ls> <ab>s.v.</ab> for some other references; but the important <ls>Dpv.</ls> 〔6.19〕 is not cited there). On the other hand, <ls>Divy</ls> <ab>ed.</ab> prints upavartanaṃ with a small initial letter; it fails to record the word in Index or Notes. I am inclined, with some dubiety, to understand the <lang>BHS</lang> word as a common noun, {%country%}. Even the <lang>Pali</lang> word may have had that meaning originally, and in the canonical passages still. Later, the <lang>Pali</lang> <ab>comms.</ab> seem clearly to have understood it as the <ab>n.</ab> of the śāl-grove; and in <ls>Dpv.</ls> 〔6.19〕 it looks like a place name. Yet, if the <lang>Chin.</lang> translation {%uncultivated%} (waste, barren) {%land%} be accepted, perhaps upavattane might be understood in this sense in that passage.
