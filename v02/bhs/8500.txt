{@nivāsayati@}¦, generally as in <lang>Skt.</lang> {%puts on%} (a garment, regularly undergarment). So also <lang>Pali</lang> nivāseti, wrongly defined in <ls>PTSD</ls> {%to dress oneself … to get clothed or dressed%}. It is true that in the common cliché (kālyam eva, or the like) nivāsayitvā, <ls>LV</ls> 〔240.11〕 (<ab>vs</ab>); <ls>Mv</ls> 〔i.34.14〕; 〔54.10〕; 〔307.14〕; 〔iii.60.3〕; 〔255.15〕; 〔272.5〕; 〔414.5〕; or nivāsya, <ls>LV</ls> 〔407.13〕; <ls>Divy</ls> 〔20.2〕; <ls>Av</ls> 〔i.290.16〕, <ab>etc.</ab>; no object is expressed. It is unnecessary to say what the monk puts on before starting his round of begging; the gerund however means strictly {%having put on%} (<ab>sc.</ab> the undergarment; <ab>cf.</ab> <ls>Prāt</ls> 〔527.8〕 cīvaraṃ nivāsayiṣyāmaḥ, with <lang>Chin.</lang> <ab>transl.</ab>; contrast prāvariṣyāmaḥ 〔529.1 ff.〕, of the upper garment; same contrast in <lang>Pali</lang>, <ls>Vin.</ls> 〔iv.185.18, 27〕). Once the verb is used in the causal sense of {%cause%} (someone else) {%to put on%} (an undergarment): <ls>Bhīk</ls> 〔11a.4〕 nivāsanaṃ nivāsayitavyā, (the nun) {%is to be caused%} (by the instructress) {%to put on the undergarment%}.
