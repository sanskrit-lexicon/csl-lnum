{@ānāpāna@}¦ (= <lang>Pali</lang> <ab>id.</ab>), {%breath%} (see below): °na-bhāvanā-vidhiḥ <ls>Mvy</ls> 〔1165〕; °na-smṛti (= <lang>Pali</lang> °na-sati), {%mindfulness%} [Page096-b] {%of breathing%}, <ls>Mvy</ls> 〔1166〕; <ls>Bbh</ls> 〔110.24〕; 〔204.26〕; 〔396.22〕; <ls>Ud</ls> 〔xv.1〕; °nānusmṛti, <ab>id.</ab>, <ls>ŚsP</ls> 〔60.8〕; on this and <ls>ŚsP</ls> 〔1443.8〕 (where text ānāpā-nusmṛtir) see <ab>s.v.</ab> {@anusmṛti@}. The word is an old dvandva; āna (= prāṇa) plus apāna (<ab>cf.</ab> <lang>Skt.</lang> prāṇāpāna, on which see <ls>G.W. Brown, JAOS</ls> 〔39.104 ff.〕). In <lang>Pali</lang> commentarial diction replaced by assāsa-passāsa (= {@āśvāsa-praśvāsa@}, <ab>q.v.</ab>). <lang>Tib.</lang> on <ls>Mvy</ls> 〔1166〕 ānāpāna (-smṛtiḥ) renders <tib>dbugs rṅub pa daṅ ḥbyuṅ ba</tib>, {%breathing in and out%}; the same or related terms are used for āśvāsa and praśvāsa. It is clear that <lang>Tib.</lang> understands āna = āśvāsa as {%inbreathing%}, apāna = praśvāsa as {%outbreathing%}. There is <lang>BHS</lang> evidence supporting this interpretation of {@āśvāsa-praśvāsa@}, <ab>q.v.</ab>
